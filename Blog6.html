The Indigenous Peoples (IPs) in Mindanao, Philippines, have long faced complex challenges rooted in historical, social, and economic factors. This essay explores the phenomenon of second-order minoritization experienced by IPs in Mindanao, focusing on how they are treated by both Muslim communities and settlers.

Mindanao has been a melting pot of diverse cultures and communities, with IPs inhabiting the region long before the arrival of Muslim and Christian settlers. Historical narratives often highlight clashes over land and resources, contributing to a complex tapestry of relationships. Understanding this historical context is crucial for comprehending the second-order minoritization of IPs.

1. Land Disputes: Land has been a central issue in the relationship between IPs and Muslim communities. The Moro Islamic Liberation Front (MILF) and the Moro National Liberation Front (MNLF), two major Muslim rebel groups, have sought autonomy in Mindanao, leading to conflicts over ancestral lands. IPs, such as the Lumad communities, often find themselves caught in the crossfire of these disputes, resulting in displacement and loss of their traditional territories.

2. Land Encroachment: Settlers, often driven by economic opportunities, have migrated to Mindanao and encroached upon IP ancestral lands. This encroachment has resulted in the displacement of IPs and the disruption of their traditional way of life. The government's promotion of agricultural expansion and infrastructure development has further exacerbated these challenges.

In conclusion, the second-order minoritization of IPs in Mindanao is a multifaceted issue. While steps have been taken to address these challenges, ongoing efforts are required to ensure the fair and equitable treatment. The path towards a more inclusive Mindanao requires collaborative efforts from all communities involved, fostering understanding, respect, and shared development.
